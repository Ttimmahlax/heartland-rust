## What Is PC/ABS?

PC/ABS is a high-performance thermoplastic blend that combines polycarbonate (PC), known for its exceptional impact resistance, heat tolerance, and optical clarity, with acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS), which contributes excellent processability, surface finish, and cost-effectiveness.

This engineered alloy delivers a unique balance of toughness, dimensional stability, and flame retardancy that pure PC or ABS alone cannot achieve, making it a material of choice for demanding electronics enclosures such as laptop housings, monitor bezels, and printer chassis.

## Why Is PC/ABS Commonly Used In Electronics?

PC/ABS is the go-to material for consumer electronics housings because it combines the exceptional impact resistance and heat deflection temperature of polycarbonate with the easy flow, lower cost, and excellent surface finish of ABS, delivering a tougher, more processable alloy than either resin alone.

Its outstanding flame retardancy—readily achieving UL 94 V-0 at thicknesses below 1 mm with both halogenated and environmentally progressive non-halogenated additives—makes it uniquely suitable for devices containing batteries and power supplies. The blend exhibits low shrinkage, minimal warpage, and excellent dimensional stability across temperature and humidity swings, ensuring that large, thin-walled parts like laptop lids and monitor bezels stay flat and maintain precise assembly gaps.

From a production perspective, PC/ABS offers a wide processing window, short cycle times, high melt stability, and strong adhesion in overmolding and ultrasonic welding, all of which reduce manufacturing costs and defects in high-volume electronics assembly.

## Why Is Natural Fiber Hard To Add To PC/ABS Formulations?

Natural fiber reinforcement is inherently challenging in PC/ABS formulations because the polymer matrix is processed at melt temperatures typically between 240–280 °C, where unprotected cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin in hemp fibers undergo rapid thermal degradation, charring, and volatile release that cause discoloration, odor, and severe loss of mechanical properties.

The high moisture absorption of natural fibers (often 6–12 % by weight) creates steam bubbles and hydrolysis risks when introduced into the low-moisture environment required by polycarbonate, leading to voids, surface defects, and embrittlement of the final part.

Poor interfacial adhesion is another critical hurdle: the hydrophilic hydroxyl groups on natural fiber surfaces have almost no chemical compatibility with the non-polar PC/ABS chains, resulting in weak fiber-matrix bonding, easy fiber pull-out, and dramatic drops in impact strength unless sophisticated coupling agents or reactive compatibilizers are employed.

Odor and VOC emissions from thermal breakdown products are particularly unacceptable in consumer electronics that undergo strict smell tests, requiring additional encapsulation or purification steps that increase cost.

Finally, the darker natural color of most bio-fibers limits their use in light-colored or high-gloss applications unless heavy pigmentation or expensive bleaching is applied, making the path from raw natural fiber to a drop-in, high-performance PC/ABS compound far more complex than simply swapping in glass.
