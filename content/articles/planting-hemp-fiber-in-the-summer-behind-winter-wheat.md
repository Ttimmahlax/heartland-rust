## What do farmers do with their land after they harvest winter wheat in July?

Winter wheat is a common crop in a farmer's rotation. Typically planted at the end of the season in fall and harvested in early summer, around July.

Depending on where in the country the farmers live, there are few options available for planting a late summer crop. In the southern part of the country it is possible to get another soybean planting in before the winter, but as you go farther north, it becomes less possible due to the amount of time required to grow.

Farmers in the north end up leaving their farmland empty until next Spring, opening the door for opportunity to plant a fast-growing crop at the end of each year.

## Can wheat farmers plant hemp fiber in summer?

July is a bustling time for wheat farmers, marking the end of one growing season and the start of another. As the golden wheat fields sway under the summer sun, farmers gear up for the harvest. This is a time of hard work but also of reward, as the combines roll through the fields, gathering the grains that have been nurtured since planting.

The wheat harvest not only represents the culmination of months of careful tending but also a crucial economic milestone for the farming community. The grain, once collected, heads off to become a part of our daily bread, pastries, and numerous other products we often take for granted.

But the work doesn't stop there for innovative farmers. As the wheat fields are cleared, they present a unique opportunity to plant industrial hemp for fiber. Hemp, known for its rapid growth and versatility, fits perfectly into this window. Within just a few months, these fields that were once golden with wheat can transform into lush green expanses of hemp fiber.

This quick turnaround is a boon for farmers looking to make the most of their land throughout the year. Hemp's fast growth rate and the shorter time frame needed to reach harvest allow farmers to add an additional crop cycle to their yearly schedule. This not only maximizes land use but also opens up new avenues for income and crop diversification, a key factor in the ever-evolving world of agriculture.

## What are the risks involved in planting hemp fiber behind a winter wheat crop?

Introducing a new crop into the rotation always comes with its share of risks and uncertainties. For farmers, the decision to diversify their crop lineup is a balance between potential benefits and the risks involved. These risks can range from unfamiliarity with the new crop's cultivation requirements to uncertainties in market demand and price fluctuations.

There's also the consideration of how the new crop will interact with the existing soil conditions and local ecosystem. Will it deplete the soil nutrients more than the usual crops? How will it affect pest and disease cycles? These are critical questions that farmers must ponder.

Adding a new crop means adapting to new cultivation practices, understanding the crop's specific needs, and sometimes even investing in new equipment—all of which require time, effort, and resources.

However, planting hemp for fiber following a winter wheat harvest can significantly mitigate these risks, offering a strategic advantage.

Firstly, hemp is known for its hardiness and adaptability, making it a less risky choice for farmers new to its cultivation. It's a crop that doesn't require extensive specialized knowledge or equipment to get started, fitting well into the existing farming practices. Secondly, the timing works beautifully with wheat's growing season.

The wheat is harvested in early summer, leaving the land ready for a second crop. Hemp, with its fast-growing nature, can be planted immediately after the wheat harvest and harvested before the winter sets in. This rotation allows farmers to make full use of their land year-round, increasing productivity without overburdening the soil.

It’s a win-win situation: the main crop of wheat is safely harvested, and hemp serves as a lucrative second crop, offering additional income without the high risk usually associated with crop diversification. This approach not only maximizes land use but also helps in maintaining soil health and fertility, as hemp is known for its beneficial effects on soil structure and nutrient cycling.

## What are the potential financial benefits of planting hemp fiber in the summer?

The financial upside of introducing hemp into the crop rotation, especially following a winter wheat harvest, is a game-changer for many farmers. Typically, after the wheat is harvested in July, the land would remain fallow until the next planting season. However, by planting hemp, farmers can turn this otherwise idle period into a productive and profitable endeavor.

Our in-house farming team has shown an additional profit of $215 per acre two years in a row on a production of 2100 acres. This represents a significant boost in annual income, especially when multiplied across larger farms. This extra income can help cover operational costs, fund new farm initiatives, or simply add to the farm's overall financial stability.

It's a smart strategy in today's agricultural industry, where maximizing the productivity and profitability of every acre is crucial. By leveraging the quick growth cycle of hemp, farmers can efficiently utilize their land year-round, ensuring that no acre lies unproductive for long.

Beyond the immediate financial benefits, planting hemp in this rotation also offers substantial soil health advantages. Hemp is known for its deep root system, which helps in breaking up soil compaction and improving soil structure. This is particularly beneficial after wheat harvest, as it prepares the soil for future crops by enhancing aeration and drainage. Moreover, hemp's roots aid in nutrient recycling, drawing up minerals and nutrients from deeper soil layers and making them available for subsequent crops.

This can reduce the need for synthetic fertilizers, leading to cost savings and a more sustainable farming practice. Additionally, hemp's rapid growth provides excellent ground cover, reducing soil erosion and helping maintain soil moisture levels. All these factors contribute to a healthier, more fertile soil, setting the stage for successful future crop cycles. By integrating hemp into their crop rotation, farmers not only gain a financial edge but also invest in the long-term health and productivity of their land.
