## What Crops Require The Most Water Consumption?

[Water-intensive crops](https://assets.wwf.org.uk/downloads/thirstycrops.pdf) significantly strain global freshwater resources, with some requiring far more irrigation than others due to their biological needs and growing conditions. Many of the modern genetics for crops have been modified to require less water or create stronger root systems to pull resources from deeper.

Rice tops the list, often demanding 2,500–3,000 liters of water per kilogram of yield, as it thrives in flooded paddies that maintain constant submersion. Sugarcane follows closely, needing around 1,500–2,000 liters per kilogram, largely because of its long growth cycle and high transpiration rates in tropical climates.

Cotton, another heavy consumer, requires approximately 1,800–2,200 liters per kilogram, driven by its deep root system and extended flowering period in arid regions. Corn, while slightly less demanding, still uses about 800–1,200 liters per kilogram, particularly during its critical pollination and grain-filling stages. Soybeans, often grown in rotation with corn, consume roughly 1,000–1,500 liters per kilogram, with peak water needs during pod development.

Almonds, a high-value crop, are notorious for requiring 15,000–20,000 liters per kilogram of shelled nuts, as orchards in dry regions like California rely heavily on irrigation. Wheat, by contrast, is moderately water-efficient, using 500–1,000 liters per kilogram, but its vast cultivation area amplifies its total water footprint.

These figures highlight why farmers are increasingly turning to low-water crops like hemp, which can thrive with as little as 300–500 liters per kilogram for fiber or grain. Adopting such crops not only conserves water but also enhances resilience in water-scarce regions.

## Why Is It Important To Introduce Low-Water Consumption Crops Into Rotation?

Introducing low-water consumption crops like hemp into crop rotations is increasingly vital due to evolving legislation, shifting export demands, and unpredictable weather patterns. Stricter water-use regulations, such as those in California’s Sustainable Groundwater Management Act, limit irrigation for high-water crops, pushing farmers to adopt alternatives that comply with legal restrictions.

Export markets are also prioritizing sustainability, with countries in Europe and Asia favoring crops like hemp grain and fiber, which require less water and align with eco-conscious trade policies. Erratic weather patterns, driven by climate change, bring prolonged droughts and irregular rainfall, making water-intensive crops like rice or cotton riskier and less viable.

Low-water crops enhance farm resilience, maintaining yields even when precipitation is scarce or irrigation is curtailed. Rotating in crops like hemp can stabilize soil moisture levels, reducing the need for supplemental watering compared to traditional rotations with corn or soybeans. This shift also supports global food security by ensuring consistent production for export amidst water scarcity.

Furthermore, low-water crops can attract government subsidies or tax incentives under new agricultural policies aimed at conservation. For instance, regions facing desertification benefit from hemp’s ability to grow with minimal irrigation, preserving arable land. Ultimately, integrating these crops into rotations aligns farming practices with environmental, economic, and legislative realities, ensuring long-term sustainability.

## Hemp Fiber and Grain Water Consumption Compared To Conventional Crops

Hemp, both for fiber and grain, stands out as a [remarkably water-efficient crop](/sustainability-news/arizona-alfalfa-farming-export-to-china-hits-a-brick-wall/) compared to many conventional crops, making it an attractive option for sustainable agriculture. Hemp fiber and grain production typically requires only 300–500 liters of water per kilogram of yield, thriving in diverse climates with minimal irrigation.

In contrast, rice demands 2,500–3,000 liters per kilogram due to its need for flooded fields, making it one of the most water-intensive crops. Cotton, often grown in arid regions, consumes 1,800–2,200 liters per kilogram, heavily reliant on irrigation systems. Corn, a common rotational crop, requires 800–1,200 liters per kilogram, particularly during its water-sensitive growth stages.

Soybeans, another major crop, use 1,000–1,500 liters per kilogram, driven by peak needs during pod formation. Almonds, a high-value but resource-heavy crop, demand an staggering 15,000–20,000 liters per kilogram of shelled nuts.

By incorporating hemp into rotations, farmers can significantly reduce their water footprint while maintaining productive yields. This comparison underscores hemp’s potential to transform farming practices in water-scarce regions, aligning with conservation goals and economic viability.

## Hemp Fiber and Grain Production To Exceed Water Replenishment Goals

Many companies, particularly in agriculture and food processing, face stringent water replenishment requirements to offset their environmental impact and comply with sustainability mandates.

These requirements, often set by regional water board commitments, push firms to restore more water to aquifers or watersheds than they consume. Converting to hemp fiber and grain production offers a strategic solution, as hemp requires significantly less water—300–500 liters per kilogram for fiber and 400–600 liters for grain—compared to crops like cotton or almonds.

For example, companies like those in the textile industry, previously reliant on water-intensive cotton (1,800–2,200 liters per kilogram), can switch to hemp fiber to drastically cut water use while meeting replenishment targets. Similarly, food and beverage companies growing water-heavy crops like rice can transition to hemp grain, reducing their water footprint and aligning with goals set by initiatives like the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals.

Hemp’s adaptability to various climates allows companies to cultivate it without over-relying on irrigation, preserving groundwater reserves. By integrating hemp into their supply chains, firms can exceed replenishment goals, as the crop’s low water needs free up resources for watershed restoration projects. This shift also enhances corporate reputations, attracting eco-conscious consumers and investors. Moreover, hemp’s fast growth cycle enables multiple harvests per year, maximizing land use efficiency while supporting water conservation.

Ultimately, adopting hemp empowers companies to surpass water replenishment requirements, fostering sustainable practices and long-term environmental resilience.
